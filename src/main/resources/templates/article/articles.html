1. Understanding Services

What is a Borewell?

A borewell is a deep, narrow hole drilled into the ground to access groundwater for various uses like drinking, irrigation, or industrial needs. It is created using drilling machines, and a pipe (called casing) is installed to protect the water source and support the structure.

Borewells are a common solution in areas where surface water is limited. The depth of a borewell can range from 100 to 1200 feet, depending on the underground water level. A submersible pump is usually installed inside the borewell to lift water to the surface.

Types of Borewell Drilling Methods

  • DTH (Down-The-Hole): Suitable for hard rock areas, uses a hammering action to break rocks.
  • In this method, the drilling vehicle is parked 150–200 feet away from the actual borewell location due to space or access limitations. Flexible hoses or rods are extended from the vehicle to the drilling spot, making it suitable for areas like narrow plots, inside farms, or residential locations with limited access.
  • Rotary: Used in soft or sandy formations, involves continuous rotation and drilling mud.
  • Calyx: Older method used for large diameter and deep wells with specific geological needs.

Choosing the Right Location

  • The success of a borewell largely depends on selecting a proper location. It is important to choose a clear and open area for drilling, where there are no underground chambers, drainage pipelines, or water tanks above or near the spot. These obstructions can damage equipment or affect the borewell structure. Avoid drilling near septic tanks or foundations, and ensure there's enough space for the boring vehicle and equipment to operate smoothly.

How to Choose the Right Borewell Contractor

  • Choosing the right borewell contractor is one of the most critical steps in ensuring the success of your borewell. Since drilling involves technical expertise, high-end machinery, and considerable investment, the contractor you select will have a direct impact on the outcome. Here are three important factors to consider:
  • Experience: An experienced contractor brings both knowledge and practical exposure to the job. Borewell drilling is not just about operating a machine; it requires understanding soil types, rock layers, and the water table. Contractors who have worked in your locality or similar terrains usually know the common depths where water can be struck and the challenges that may arise during drilling. Always ask about the contractor’s years of service and request a list of projects they have handled in your area.
  • Equipment: Modern borewell drilling relies heavily on the type and quality of equipment. Outdated or poorly maintained rigs may not be able to drill deep enough or may take longer, increasing costs. Before finalizing a contractor, check what kind of drilling rigs they use (e.g., rotary, percussion, DTH rigs) and whether their equipment can handle the depth and soil/rock conditions in your region. Well-equipped contractors can complete the job more efficiently, with less risk of damage to property or incomplete drilling.
  • Reviews and Reputation: Word of mouth and reviews are strong indicators of a contractor’s reliability. Ask neighbors, community members, or even local water authorities for feedback on contractors they have used. Positive reviews reflect not just successful borewell completion but also honesty in pricing, timeliness, and after-service support. A reputed contractor is also more likely to guide you correctly about casing pipes, motor installation, and long-term maintenance.
